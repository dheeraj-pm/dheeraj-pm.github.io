Challenge Microsoft Teams, a widely-used collaboration platform, faced significant accessibility challenges for color-blind users. This underserved group, representing roughly 10% of Teams' 280 million users (28 million people), struggled with color-coded information, low contrast, and inaccessible data visualizations. Our challenge was to enhance Teams’ accessibility for this user group without disrupting the broader user experience.
Results Our team won first place out of 10 competing teams, selected by Microsoft stakeholders. We designed a solution that addressed the core accessibility challenges of color-blind users, proposing features that improved the user experience while aligning with Microsoft’s inclusivity and product goals.

  • Defining the product vision and scope
  • Conducting research on user needs and accessibility challenges
  • Leading the prioritization process using MoSCoW
  • Coordinating cross-functional collaboration with designers and researchers
  • Presenting the final solution to Microsoft stakeholders

Discovery and Ideation

Identify the Problem:

Our goal was to make Microsoft Teams more accessible to color-blind users, a group often left behind when it comes to digital accessibility. Given that 10% of Teams' 280 million users (28 million people) are color-blind, they face challenges like distinguishing color-coded information and dealing with low contrast visuals, making their Teams experience less effective.

Research:

To estimate the impact, we conducted research and calculated that around 28 million Teams users are color-blind. This was achieved through secondary research and discovery techniques since we didn’t have access to proprietary Microsoft data. We researched color blindness statistics and utilized simulations to understand the experience of color-blind users within Teams.
Key statistics included:

  • Global color blindness affects 300 million people.
  • 72% of Teams users are men, and 1 in 8 men are color-blind.

Brainstorm Ideas:

After identifying the issues faced by color-blind users, we brainstormed potential solutions such as:

  • Creating an onboarding workflow for accessibility settings.
  • Enhancing high-contrast modes.
  • Developing alternative visual cues to replace color-coded information.
  • Improving data visualizations to be accessible to all.

Defining the Product Vision

Create a Product Vision:

Our product vision was to make Microsoft Teams a more inclusive tool for collaboration by enabling a seamless and accessible experience for color-blind users, ensuring they can navigate the platform without relying solely on color cues.

Define the Product Roadmap:

We defined a roadmap that prioritized quick wins and high-impact changes. The core milestones included:

  • Initial user research and problem discovery.
  • Design and implementation of customizable accessibility features.
  • User testing with color-blind individuals.
  • Final presentation of our solutions to Microsoft stakeholders.

Prioritization and Backlog Management

Prioritize Features:

Using the MoSCoW method, we focused on Must-Have features for our Minimum Viable Product (MVP):

  • Must Have: Accessibility onboarding, alternative visual cues, improved high-contrast mode.
  • Should Have: Enhanced customization options for users to tweak accessibility settings.
  • Could Have: Integration of accessible data visualizations into Microsoft Teams' charts and graphs.

Manage the Product Backlog:

We created a backlog of features prioritized based on feasibility, user impact, and alignment with Microsoft's business goals. The backlog included features addressing visual issues, user settings, and customization.

Design and Development

Collaborate with Design Team:

Working closely with our designers, we created wireframes for the new accessibility features. The design focused on:

  • Onboarding Workflow: An accessible user journey from the first use of Microsoft Teams, allowing users to customize settings to their needs.
  • High-Contrast Mode: Improving existing modes to work more seamlessly, providing visual clarity without losing key design elements.
  • Alternative Visual Cues: Introducing shapes and text labels for color-coded actions.

Define Specifications:

We defined clear specifications for our developers, ensuring accessibility settings were embedded in Teams' core functionality. The specs outlined specific color palettes, high-contrast adjustments, and the introduction of shape-based markers for actions that previously relied solely on color.

Oversee Development:

We worked iteratively, ensuring that each feature aligned with the roadmap and addressed user pain points. Despite resource constraints, we ensured designs reflected the needs of color-blind users.

Testing and Quality Assurance

Conduct Testing:

To validate our solutions, we simulated color-blindness using browser plugins and tested Microsoft Teams’ interface. This helped us identify issues with color-coded areas and visual elements that were not accessible.

Gather Feedback:

We gathered feedback from simulated environments and online communities, where we interacted with color-blind individuals. This feedback guided further iterations of our design.

Launch and Post-Launch

Prepare for Launch:

Since we did not have the means to fully implement the changes, our designs were ready to be integrated by Microsoft Teams' development team. We prepared a comprehensive presentation that outlined our proposed features, designs, and metrics for success.

Monitor Performance:

Post-launch, we suggested tracking key metrics such as:

  • Increase in accessibility settings usage.
  • Improvement in the experience for color-blind users (measured through user feedback and support tickets).
  • A potential reduction in task completion time for color-blind users navigating the platform.

Iterate and Improve:

Our roadmap included future iterations based on ongoing user feedback. The flexibility of the design allowed for incremental improvements as more data on user behavior became available.

Key Responsibilities Throughout the Process:
  • Customer Focus: Ensuring our design solutions addressed the real problems of color-blind users while maintaining the integrity of the product.
  • Strategic Thinking: Aligning accessibility improvements with Microsoft’s long-term vision of inclusivity.
  • Collaboration: Closely working with cross-functional teams, including designers, researchers, and developers.
  • Data-Driven Decision-Making: Using secondary data, simulations, and user feedback to guide our design and prioritization decisions.
Additional Considerations:
  • Agile Methodologies: We adopted an agile approach, iterating quickly based on feedback and adjusting our roadmap where needed.
  • Product-Market Fit: Our final solution was aligned with the needs of color-blind users and Microsoft’s broader mission of accessibility, ensuring a high degree of product-market fit.
  • Continuous Improvement: We recommended ongoing refinements to accessibility features based on user feedback and evolving market needs.
